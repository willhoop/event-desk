Polls are not probabilities

A candidate polling 29% two years out does not have a 29% chance of winning. Early national primary polls mostly measure name recognition. History's early leaders, roughly two years before the primaries:

Early leaders who lost the nomination: Lieberman 2004, Clinton 2008, Giuliani 2008 (R), Jeb Bush 2016 (R). Big names, big early numbers, no nomination.
Early leaders who won it: Romney 2012 (R), Clinton 2016, Biden 2020, Trump 2024 (R).
The base rate: the early national leader has won roughly half the time in the modern era. A 29-point poll share is worth much less than 29% probability — and a 2-point share can be worth more than 2%, if the candidate has a real path the public hasn't seen yet.

The betting strategy

Rule 1 — a divergence is information, not automatically a trade. The market knows polls exist. When price and polls disagree, the market is usually pricing something polls cannot see: an announcement, a scandal, momentum, or the historical fact that early poll leads melt. Bet only when you can name the thing the market has wrong.
Harris — polls 29%, market ~8¢. The market says her poll lead is a name-recognition ghost: she is the most famous Democrat alive, and famous names top early polls then fade (Lieberman, Giuliani). If you think the party would actually renominate her, the market is paying 11-to-1 against the polling leader. That is the boldest fade on the board.
Ossoff — polls ~2%, market ~14¢. The reverse case. The market is pricing an announcement that polling cannot measure yet. But Ossoff told CNN directly: "I am not running for president in 2028." A 14¢ price on a candidate polling at 2% who says he is not running is the market pricing a change of heart. Our desk's wedge trade — nominated but loses — inherits this risk.
AOC — polls ~10%, market ~15¢. Market slightly ahead of polls, consistent with her momentum in surveys since February. The cheapest disagreement on the board — and the one Nate Silver flagged early.
Buttigieg — polls ~13%, market ~5¢. Second-biggest gap in the polls' favor. He leads several likely-voter polls outright (Emerson had him first). If the market is right, it thinks his support is broad but shallow. If polls are right, 5¢ is the value bet on this page.
Newsom — polls ~17%, market ~18¢. Polls and money agree. No edge; the market has him fairly priced. This row is the control group that makes the other rows meaningful.
Rule 2 — size it like a bet, not a belief. Divergence trades are long shots by construction. Quarter-Kelly or less, and never more than your theme cap. The Ossoff desk's calculator applies this automatically.

How this average works — the 538 lineage

1 · Recency. Each poll decays 3% per day of age (538 uses ~7%/day near election day; two years out, we decay gentler so the average is stable). A 30-day-old poll counts about 40% as much as a fresh one.
2 · Sample size. Weight scales with the square root of sample size, capped at 5,000 — same cap 538 uses — so one mega-poll cannot swamp the average.
3 · Pollster quality. Each firm gets a weight from its approximate 538 star rating (accuracy plus transparency). Emerson, Echelon, YouGov and AtlasIntel count near full weight; low-transparency or partisan shops count much less.
4 · Population. Likely-voter polls get full weight, registered-voter polls 90%, all-adult polls 75% — 538's "narrowest population" preference, expressed as weights.
5 · House effects. For every pollster we measure how far its numbers habitually sit from the rest of the field, per candidate, and subtract half of that lean before averaging — the same half-correction logic 538 describes. AtlasIntel's huge Buttigieg numbers and Harvard-Harris's huge Harris numbers get pulled toward the field.
6 · What we do not do. No herding penalty, no bootstrap, no imputation — those need 538's full historical database. The skeleton is theirs; the muscle is what fits in one page.
