02Polls vs the marketthe money chart

This is the desk's product — and it does not cheat the units. A poll share is not a probability, so we never subtract one from a price. Instead we fitted a logistic model on 90 candidacies from 20 contested primaries, 1972–2024 — each candidate's early national poll share against whether they actually won the nomination. The model (win odds rise with the log of your share of the field; leaders convert ~55% historically; it beats the base-rate Brier score 0.150 to 0.173) turns today's shares into a poll-implied price in cents, scaled so the listed field sums to 95% — leaving 5% for a nominee nobody is polling yet, which is what Carter 1976 was. The edge column is market minus implied: cents against cents.

03The average41 polls, one number

Every national 2028 Democratic primary poll from 2026, blended the way FiveThirtyEight does it: newer polls count more (3% less per day of age), bigger samples count more (square root of sample size), better pollsters count more (our own E4 score — see the scoreboard below), likely-voter polls beat registered-voter polls, and each pollster's habitual lean — its house effect — is measured and half-corrected before averaging.

04Polls are not probabilitieshistory's warning

A candidate polling 29% two years out does not have a 29% chance of winning. Early national primary polls mostly measure name recognition. History's early leaders, roughly two years before the primaries:

Early leaders who lost the nomination: Lieberman 2004, Clinton 2008, Giuliani 2008 (R), Jeb Bush 2016 (R). Big names, big early numbers, no nomination.
Early leaders who won it: Romney 2012 (R), Clinton 2016, Biden 2020, Trump 2024 (R).
The base rate: the early national leader has won roughly half the time in the modern era. A 29-point poll share is worth much less than 29% probability — and a 2-point share can be worth more than 2%, if the candidate has a real path the public hasn't seen yet.
05The strategytwo rules, four cases

Two rules, then the four live cases they apply to.

Rule 1 — a divergence is information, not automatically a trade. The market knows polls exist. When price and polls disagree, the market is usually pricing something polls cannot see: an announcement, a scandal, momentum, or the historical fact that early poll leads melt. Bet only when you can name the thing the market has wrong.
Rule 2 — bet small on purpose. There is a formula (the Kelly criterion) that computes the biggest bet the math can justify given your edge. We never bet more than a quarter of what it says — because every number you feed it is a guess, and betting full Kelly on a wrong guess is how bankrolls die. In practice: if a bet feels big, halve it. Then halve it again.

The four cases on today's board

Harris — polls 29%, market ~8¢. The market says her poll lead is a name-recognition ghost: she is the most famous Democrat alive, and famous names top early polls then fade (Lieberman, Giuliani). If you think the party would actually renominate her, the market is paying 11-to-1 against the polling leader. That is the boldest fade on the board.
Ossoff — polls ~2%, market ~14¢. The reverse case. The market is pricing an announcement that polling cannot measure yet. Ossoff told CNN directly: "I am not running for president in 2028" — but denials this early are nearly worthless as evidence. Obama told Meet the Press in January 2006, "I will not run for president or vice president in 2008," and announced thirteen months later. The market is not being naive at 14¢; it is remembering 2006. The real question is whether Ossoff is Obama-like enough to justify it.
AOC — polls ~10%, market ~15¢. Market slightly ahead of polls, consistent with her momentum in surveys since February. The cheapest disagreement on the board — and the one Nate Silver flagged early.
Buttigieg — polls ~13%, market ~5¢. Second-biggest gap in the polls' favor. He leads several likely-voter polls outright (Emerson had him first). If the market is right, it thinks his support is broad but shallow. If polls are right, 5¢ is the value bet on this page.
Newsom — polls ~17%, market ~18¢. Polls and money agree. No edge; the market has him fairly priced. This row is the control group that makes the other rows meaningful.

Distance from consensus = average gap, in points, between this firm's numbers and the field's weighted average (its house effect). Big distance is not proof of error — but with no elections to grade against until 2028, agreement with the crowd is the only accuracy proxy the data offers, so persistent outliers earn less weight, not zero.

07Methodologythe 538 lineage, and sources

How this average works — the 538 lineage

1 · Recency. Each poll decays 3% per day of age (538 uses ~7%/day near election day; two years out, we decay gentler so the average is stable). A 30-day-old poll counts about 40% as much as a fresh one.
2 · Sample size. Weight scales with the square root of sample size, capped at 5,000 — same cap 538 uses — so one mega-poll cannot swamp the average.
3 · Pollster quality — scored by our own engine. 538 shut down in March 2025, so its ratings froze; we use its framework as inspiration but compute our own E4 score from this page's poll database: 40% distance-from-consensus (the firm's measured house effect), 20% sample-size discipline, 10% likely-voter discipline, and a 30% penalty for documented partisan clientele. The full scoreboard is published above; every input is auditable. Nate Silver's Silver Bulletin still updates independent ratings and is our external cross-check.
4 · Population. Likely-voter polls get full weight, registered-voter polls 90%, all-adult polls 75% — 538's "narrowest population" preference, expressed as weights.
5 · House effects. For every pollster we measure how far its numbers habitually sit from the rest of the field, per candidate, and subtract half of that lean before averaging — the same half-correction logic 538 describes. AtlasIntel's huge Buttigieg numbers and Harvard-Harris's huge Harris numbers get pulled toward the field.
6 · What we do not do. No herding penalty, no bootstrap, no imputation — those need 538's full historical database, which stopped updating when ABC closed 538 in 2025. The skeleton is theirs; the muscle is what fits in one page; the upkeep is ours.
