Buy now, or wait for your price?

Market order = "buy it right now at whatever it costs" — instant, pays the fee. Limit order = "I will pay 14¢ and not a penny more" — your offer waits in a queue until someone sells at your price. No fee, better price, but it may never fill. We pre-answer both questions from the last month of real prices — override only if you know something the tape does not:

1 · How jumpy is this market today?
2 · Chance it runs off before your limit fills?

About the "rewards" popups: those pay market-makers to park orders all day. At personal size they earn pennies while pros take the pool — and one bad fill costs days of rewards. Use limit orders to skip the fee, not to farm rewards.

Before anything else: is it even investable?

Our house rule. A market has to pass all seven checks below or it does not go on this board, no matter how good the price looks.

1. The outcome is a hard fact, not an opinion. Someone wins the election or they don't. We skip markets that resolve on "credible reporting," vague wording, or a judgment call by the site.
2. The rules spell out every ending — including ties and cancellations. Before we bet, we read the resolution rules and confirm they say exactly what happens on a tie, a voided or cancelled event, a postponed date, or a candidate dropping out. If an ending isn't covered in writing, a "weird" result can mean a refund at best or a loss at worst. Never assume — most markets resolve NO (not refund) when the thing simply doesn't happen.
3. Nobody trading can rig the result. Millions of voters decide an election — that passes. "Will this person tweet by Friday" fails, because the person can see the market and act. Small-committee awards and obscure decisions fail too.
4. One official source decides, and the rules name it. Certified election results, an official government number. If two sources could disagree, the market can end in a dispute.
5. There is a hard deadline. You know the worst-case date your money comes back.
6. You can get out early. Enough daily volume that selling before resolution is realistic, not theoretical.
7. The venue itself will still be standing. Regulation and counterparty risk — covered in the Arbitrage & risk section below.

The Ossoff markets pass: certified election outcomes, named sources, fixed dates, deep volume. The tie/void fine print for each one is linked from its card.

How this all works, in 60 seconds

1 · Price = probability. A "Yes" share costing 14¢ pays $1 if the thing happens. So the market thinks: 14% chance.

2 · You can bet no. Betting something WON'T happen costs (100 − price)¢ and pays $1 if it doesn't.

3 · Two ways to buy. A market order buys instantly at the going price and pays a small fee. A limit order names your own price and waits in line — free, but may never fill.

4 · You can sell anytime. No need to wait years — sell whenever the price has moved your way.

5 · Two stores sell these. Kalshi (US-regulated, your payout is guaranteed by a clearinghouse, cash held at big banks) and Polymarket (cheaper, but crypto-based — you trust code and your own wallet). Neither is FDIC insured.

6 · Taxes exist. Profits are taxable. There's no IRS rule for these yet; most defensible route is capital-gains treatment with clean records — use "download my bets" on the My bets tab and talk to a CPA. Not tax advice.

7 · The golden rule. Every bet here rises and falls on one person's fortunes. Keep the whole theme under ~5% of your money (set yours in ⚙).
